10 FAQs of National Parks.

1. Yellowstone National Park.
Located in Wyoming, Idaho, and Montana is the oldest U.S. national park, founded in 1872.
2. The second U.S. national park created is no longer a national park.
Mackinac National Park in Michigan was established in 1875 and was returned to the state in 1895. It is now one of Michigan's State Parks.
3. National parks are home to life on a big scale.
Sequoia trees are some of the world's largest living things, and Alaskan brown bears are some of the world's largest carnivores.
4. California's Sequoia National Park.
Is home to the largest living single-stem tree in the world. The wonderfully named General Sherman. The tree is approximately 275 feet tall and weighs approximately 1,900 metric tons.
5. Mammoth Cave National Park.
Located in Kentucky, has the longest cave system in the world with more than 3,454 mapped miles.
6. Ribbon Falls in Yosemite.
Is nine times taller than Niagara Falls.
7. Wrangell-St. Elias National Park, Alaska.
Is the largest national park, covering 13,000 square miles or 13.2 million acres.
8. Death Valley National Park.
Is the largest national park in the lower 48 U.S. states, stretching 5,300 square miles or 3.4 million acres.
9. Hot Springs National Park in Arkansas.
The smallest national park, coming in at about 5,500 acres. This was actually the first federally protected piece of land in 1832. It was not named a national park until 1921.
10. Trail Ridge Road in Rocky Mountain National Park.
connects Grand Lake and Estes Park, is the highest continuously paved road in the U.S., and it includes breathtaking views and vistas along its entire 48 miles.

National Parks in Texas

Big Thicket

  • Located in Beaumont, TX
  • Historical size of the Big Thicket region: roughly 3.5 million acres
  • Size of Big Thicket National Preserve: 113,114 acres
  • Number of plant species: 1,320 (currently known)

Guadalupe Mountains

  • Located in Fort Davis, TX
  • The highest peak in the Guadalupe Mountains is appropriately named Guadalupe Peak. At 8,751 feet it is the highest point in the entire state of Texas.
  • Archaeological evidence suggests that humans have been living in the Guadalupe Mountains region for at least 10,000 years.
  • The Mescalero Apaches lived in the area until the 1800s when the American Civil War ended.
